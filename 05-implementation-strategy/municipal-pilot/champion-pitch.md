# The Champion Pitch (One Page)

*Bring this to one local official with authority over city data — ideally the head of economic development, the open-data/transparency office, or a councilmember running on local business. Frame everything as their win, not ours.*

---

## The offer, in one sentence

**Publish one dataset your city already collects, as a monthly aggregate, and we'll build a free public dashboard that makes your local economy visible — at near-zero cost and no new bureaucracy.**

## What you (the city) get

- **A real-time picture of your local economy.** Where are businesses opening and closing? Which neighborhoods are growing? Today that information sits in a database nobody looks at. We turn it into a map your office — and your residents — can read.
- **Transparency credit that costs you almost nothing.** You already collect this data. Publishing it is a disclosure, not a new program, not a new hire, not new software you have to run.
- **An economic-development tool.** Business-formation and permit trends by neighborhood are exactly the signals your office needs to target support, recruit investment, and show results.
- **A defensible, non-partisan win.** It's just facts about your own city. There's nothing to attack — no interpretation, no spin, no editorial. The data speaks; you don't have to.

## What we ask of you

- **One dataset to start** — our recommendation is **business registrations** (new and closed), which is clearly economic, neighborhood-level, and not sensitive. One dataset, not ten.
- **Monthly aggregates** published to a public, downloadable location (your existing open-data portal, or a public repo). Aggregates only — counts by neighborhood and sector, **never individual records**.
- **A point of contact** on your staff who can export the data once a month, or a one-time setup so it exports itself.

That's the entire ask. No budget line. No procurement. No vendor lock-in.

## What we do (free)

- Define the small, open data standard with you (it's two pages — see the micro-standard).
- Recruit and supervise a university team to build the publication pipeline and the public dashboard.
- Document the methodology publicly so anyone can audit or reproduce it.
- Host the dashboard. You publish the data; we make it legible.

## The guardrails (so this is safe to say yes to)

- **Aggregates only.** Counts by area and category, with small cells suppressed. No person, no business, no address is ever identifiable. This is not surveillance — it's the opposite of it.
- **You publish; we don't gatekeep.** The data lives in the open, mirrored and forkable. It belongs to the city and the public, not to us.
- **It outlives all of us.** Because it's public and mirrored, a future administration can't quietly delete the record. That permanence is a feature — it's what makes the transparency credible.
- **We publish the unflattering months too.** If business closures spike, that number goes up like any other. A measurement you can trust is one that doesn't flinch. We commit to that up front, in writing — and so should you, because it's exactly what makes the data believable.

## Why this matters beyond your city

This is the first instance of a simple, repeatable standard for making local economies visible. If it works here, it becomes a template other cities adopt. **You're not just publishing a dataset — you're the proof that this can be done.** That's a leadership story, and it's yours.
