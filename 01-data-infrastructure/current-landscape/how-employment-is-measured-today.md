# How Employment Is Measured Today

## Introduction

In a developed country, employment measurement is a problem of lag and precision -- the data exists but arrives slowly and gets revised. In Venezuela and Colombia, the problem is fundamentally different: **much of the labor market is invisible to statistics entirely.** When 40-60% of workers operate in the informal economy, when a quarter of the population has emigrated, when a medical doctor drives a taxi to survive, the very concept of "employment" as measured by traditional surveys breaks down.

This document maps how employment is measured in Venezuela and Colombia, what the measurement systems miss, and where modern data sources could fill the gap. The US Bureau of Labor Statistics methodology is included as a reference point -- not because it is the target, but because it illustrates what a well-resourced statistical system looks like and, by contrast, how vast the measurement deficit is in Latin American developing economies.

The stakes are not abstract. A Venezuelan deciding whether to migrate to Colombia, Peru, or Chile is making that decision almost blind. A Colombian employer in Cucuta trying to hire from a population that includes hundreds of thousands of Venezuelan migrants has no data on available skills. A government attempting to design social programs has no reliable count of who is working, at what, or for how much. econOS exists because the information gap in these labor markets is not a minor inefficiency -- it is a structural failure that damages millions of lives.

## 1. What "Employment" Means in Developing Countries

### 1.1 The Concept Breaks Down

Standard employment measurement rests on a taxonomy designed for formal economies: a person is employed, unemployed, or not in the labor force. "Employed" means working for pay during a reference week. "Unemployed" means not working but actively seeking work. These categories, codified by the International Labour Organization (ILO), were built for economies where the majority of work happens through registered employer-employee relationships, where "seeking work" means submitting applications, and where the boundary between employment and unemployment is reasonably clear.

In Venezuela and Colombia, these boundaries dissolve. Consider the following situations, all commonplace:

- A petroleum engineer who lost his job when PDVSA collapsed now sells coffee from a cart on the street in Caracas. He earns money, so he is "employed." But he is not using his skills, his income is a fraction of his former salary, and he has no employment contract, no social security contributions, and no employer to report him on any survey. His work is invisible to the establishment side of any labor survey.

- A teacher in Maracaibo earns a government salary equivalent to a few dollars per month. She supplements this by selling empanadas in the evening and tutoring on weekends. She holds three "jobs," but her primary formal employment is essentially fictitious in economic terms -- the real salary does not cover basic food needs. The informal work that actually sustains her family is unmeasured.

- A Colombian day laborer in Bucaramanga works construction three days one week, nothing the next, and does motorcycle deliveries through Rappi when construction work dries up. He is never "unemployed" in the survey sense -- he always did something in the reference week -- but he has no stable employment, no contract, and no visibility in any administrative data.

- A Venezuelan in Bogota works as a street vendor without legal migration status. He is part of the Colombian labor market in every real sense, but he is unlikely to appear in surveys (he may avoid interviewers) and cannot appear in any administrative records because he has no work permit.

The ILO definition of employment -- any work for pay or profit during the reference week, even one hour -- technically captures all of these people as "employed." But the label is meaningless for understanding their economic reality. The headline employment rate in a country where half the "employed" population is engaged in subsistence-level informal survival work tells you almost nothing about the health of the labor market.

### 1.2 Informality as the Default

In high-income OECD countries, informal employment is an exception -- estimated at 5-15% of total employment. In Venezuela and Colombia, it is the norm. The ILO and national statistical agencies define informal employment generally as work that is not covered by labor legislation, social protection, or income taxation. In practice, this means workers without written contracts, without social security enrollment, and without employer-side reporting obligations.

**Venezuela:** Estimates of informal employment range from 40% to over 60% of total employment, depending on the source and the year. The Venezuelan Observatory of Finance (OVF) and ENCOVI (Encuesta Nacional de Condiciones de Vida), a multi-university household survey, have both placed informality well above 50% in recent years. During the peak of the economic crisis (2017-2021), formal employment collapsed as private companies closed or shrank and public sector real wages became negligible. Many formally employed workers in the public sector are "employed" in name only -- they appear on a government payroll but earn so little that they must work informally to survive.

**Colombia:** DANE (Departamento Administrativo Nacional de Estadistica) reports that informal employment accounts for approximately 56% of employment in the 13 largest metropolitan areas and roughly 47% nationally as of recent GEIH measurements. Unlike Venezuela, Colombia has a functioning statistical system that actually measures informality regularly, but the measurement itself reveals the scale of the problem: nearly half of all workers are outside the formal economy.

The implication for measurement is stark. Traditional establishment surveys -- the kind where you survey registered businesses about their payrolls -- can only see the formal economy. In the US, this misses perhaps 5-10% of economic activity. In Venezuela, it misses the majority.

### 1.3 Underemployment and Skills Mismatch

The gap between "having work" and "having adequate work" is enormous in crisis economies. The ILO distinguishes between:

- **Time-related underemployment**: Working fewer hours than desired
- **Inadequate employment situations**: Occupational mismatch (skills), income inadequacy, excessive hours

In Venezuela, the dominant phenomenon is income-inadequate employment. People work full-time or more, but their earnings do not cover basic needs. ENCOVI surveys have found that a large majority of Venezuelan workers earn below the poverty line. A surgeon who drives Uber, a professor who runs a food stand, an engineer who repairs phones -- these are not "underemployed" in the traditional time-based sense. They work long hours. But the economic system has destroyed the return on their human capital.

Colombia faces a related but less extreme version. The "rebusque" -- the daily hustle of combining informal activities to survive -- is a cultural and economic reality for millions. DANE captures some of this through underemployment questions in the GEIH, but the full picture of skills mismatch and income inadequacy is difficult to survey.

## 2. How Employment Is Measured in Venezuela

### 2.1 The Institutional Framework

Venezuela's official labor statistics are the responsibility of the **Instituto Nacional de Estadistica (INE)**, which was established in 2001 to replace the older OCEI (Oficina Central de Estadistica e Informatica). The INE is nominally independent but operates under the executive branch and has been subject to significant political pressure, particularly since the economic crisis intensified after 2013.

The primary labor survey instrument was the **Encuesta de Hogares por Muestreo (EHM)** -- a continuous household survey modeled on ILO recommendations. The EHM collected data on labor force participation, employment, unemployment, informality, income, hours worked, and related indicators. At its best, the EHM covered approximately 40,000-45,000 households per semester and produced national and state-level estimates.

### 2.2 The Collapse of Official Statistics

Starting around 2014-2015, Venezuela's official statistical system began to deteriorate:

**Publication gaps:** The INE stopped publishing labor market data with any regularity. The Encuesta de Hogares data, which had been published semi-annually, became sporadic and then largely ceased. The INE website went through periods where data was unavailable or clearly outdated. The BCV (Banco Central de Venezuela) similarly stopped publishing key economic indicators for extended periods between 2014 and 2019.

**Political interference:** The government had strong incentives to suppress unfavorable data. As hyperinflation set in (reaching an estimated 130,000% in 2018 by IMF calculations), unemployment rose, and the economy contracted by an estimated 75% of GDP between 2013 and 2021, accurate statistics would have contradicted the official narrative. Labor statistics were no exception. When the government did release numbers, they were widely viewed as unreliable by independent economists, international organizations, and Venezuelans themselves.

**Methodological deterioration:** Even when data was collected, the quality of fieldwork suffered. INE lost experienced staff to emigration (the brain drain affected the statistical agency itself). Survey response rates likely declined as conditions worsened. The ability to conduct representative sampling across the country was compromised by infrastructure breakdown, security concerns in some regions, and resource constraints.

**The ENCOVI alternative:** In the absence of credible official data, a consortium of Venezuelan universities -- led by the Universidad Catolica Andres Bello (UCAB), Universidad Central de Venezuela (UCV), and Universidad Simon Bolivar (USB) -- began conducting the **Encuesta Nacional de Condiciones de Vida (ENCOVI)** in 2014. ENCOVI is an independent household survey that covers living conditions including employment, income, nutrition, health, and education. It has been conducted annually (with some gaps) and has become the most cited source on Venezuelan social and economic conditions.

ENCOVI's labor market findings have consistently painted a picture far worse than any official statistics:

- Informal employment exceeding 50% and rising
- Real wages collapsing to single-digit monthly dollar equivalents
- Widespread multi-activity survival strategies
- Massive labor force exit through emigration

However, ENCOVI has limitations: its sample size is smaller than what a well-funded national statistical office could achieve (typically 10,000-15,000 households), its frequency is annual at best, and it cannot provide sub-national granularity for most indicators.

### 2.3 What the Crisis Did to the Labor Market (and Its Measurement)

The Venezuelan economic crisis was not a normal recession. It was a structural collapse that fundamentally altered the labor market in ways that standard measurement cannot capture:

**Formal sector destruction:** Thousands of private businesses closed. FEDECAMARAS (the Venezuelan federation of chambers of commerce) reported that the number of active companies fell by more than half between 2006 and 2020. The formal private sector shrank catastrophically, taking with it the employer-employee relationships that establishment surveys measure.

**Public sector hollowing:** The government maintained nominally large public employment, but real wages fell to nearly zero. A public school teacher's monthly salary fell to the equivalent of $2-5 at various points during the crisis. Workers technically remained employed but were functionally destitute. Many stopped showing up for work, working informally instead, while remaining on payrolls for whatever marginal benefit (access to the CLAP food box program, for instance) formal employment still provided.

**Dollarization of the informal economy:** As the bolivar became worthless, much of the informal economy shifted to dollar-denominated transactions. This created a dual economy: a formal sector still operating in rapidly depreciating bolivares (and therefore statistically visible but economically meaningless) and an informal dollar-based economy that was economically real but statistically invisible.

**Emigration:** The single largest labor market shock was the departure of over 7 million Venezuelans -- roughly 25% of the pre-crisis population. This is discussed in detail in Section 5, but the measurement implication is immediate: the population denominator used for calculating employment rates, unemployment rates, and labor force participation rates became unreliable. If INE continued using pre-crisis population projections (as it appears to have done for some calculations), all labor market rates were distorted by an incorrect base.

### 2.4 Current State of Venezuelan Employment Data

As of the mid-2020s, the situation is:

- **INE** publishes some labor market data intermittently, but its credibility remains low among independent analysts, international organizations, and the Venezuelan academic community.
- **BCV** has resumed some data publication, but employment is not its primary focus.
- **ENCOVI** remains the most trusted source but is annual, limited in sample size, and does not provide the real-time or even monthly data that a functioning economy needs.
- **International organizations** (IMF, World Bank, ILO, ECLAC) publish estimates for Venezuela, but these are often modeled or extrapolated due to the lack of reliable primary data. The ILO's ILOSTAT database, for example, notes data limitations for Venezuela on most labor indicators.
- **The Venezuelan Observatory of Finance (OVF)** and other independent Venezuelan research organizations produce analysis, but they face the same underlying data scarcity.

The net result: **no one knows, with reasonable precision, how many Venezuelans are employed, in what activities, at what earnings, or under what conditions.** This is not a data lag problem. It is a data void.

## 3. How Employment Is Measured in Colombia

### 3.1 The Institutional Framework

Colombia's statistical infrastructure is substantially stronger than Venezuela's. The **Departamento Administrativo Nacional de Estadistica (DANE)** is a respected institution with a long track record and a significantly larger budget and staff than most Latin American statistical agencies. DANE has maintained continuous data production through political transitions and economic disruptions, and its methodologies generally follow international standards.

The primary labor market instrument is the **Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares (GEIH)** -- the Large Integrated Household Survey. The GEIH is a continuous monthly survey that has been running in its current form since 2006, replacing the earlier Encuesta Continua de Hogares (ECH).

### 3.2 GEIH Methodology

**Coverage:** The GEIH covers 13 major metropolitan areas monthly and extends to departmental (state-level) capitals and rural areas on a rotating quarterly basis. The 13 metropolitan areas covered monthly are: Bogota, Medellin, Cali, Barranquilla, Bucaramanga, Manizales, Pasto, Pereira, Cucuta, Ibague, Monteria, Cartagena, and Villavicencio. Together, these areas account for roughly 50% of Colombia's population.

**Sample size:** Approximately 23,000 households per month in the 13 cities, expanding to roughly 62,000 households per quarter when including departmental capitals and rural areas. This is a substantial sample by Latin American standards.

**Methodology:** The GEIH uses a probabilistic, multi-stage, stratified sampling design. Interviews are conducted continuously throughout the month (not confined to a single reference week as in the US CPS). The reference period for employment status is the week preceding the interview. DANE uses CAPI (Computer Assisted Personal Interviewing) and has increasingly incorporated CATI (telephone) methods, particularly since COVID-19 disrupted in-person fieldwork.

**What it measures:**
- Employment, unemployment, and inactivity rates
- Formal vs. informal employment (using both the enterprise-based and the job-based ILO definitions)
- Employment by sector (agriculture, industry, commerce, services, construction, etc.)
- Employment by occupational position (employer, self-employed, private employee, government employee, unpaid family worker, domestic worker, day laborer)
- Hours worked
- Earnings (for wage workers and self-employed)
- Underemployment (subjective and objective measures)
- Health insurance and pension coverage (key informality indicators)

**Release lag:** DANE publishes the monthly GEIH labor market bulletin approximately 30-45 days after the reference month. The January data, for example, typically arrives in late February or early March.

### 3.3 Strengths of the Colombian System

Relative to Venezuela and most of Latin America, Colombia's employment measurement has genuine strengths:

**Continuity:** The GEIH has operated without interruption for nearly two decades, providing consistent time series.

**Informality measurement:** DANE explicitly measures informality using two complementary definitions -- the "enterprise" definition (workers in unregistered businesses or businesses with fewer than 5 employees that are not registered) and the "social security" definition (workers not contributing to health insurance or pension). This dual measurement captures different dimensions of informality and allows for more nuanced analysis.

**Frequency and timeliness:** Monthly publication with a 30-45 day lag is reasonable by developing-country standards, though still slow relative to what modern data sources could provide.

**Methodological transparency:** DANE publishes detailed methodological documentation, microdata for academic use, and metadata through its website and through the ANDA (Accelerated National Data Archive) catalog.

**PILA integration:** Colombia's PILA (Planilla Integrada de Liquidacion de Aportes) is a unified system for social security contributions. All formal employers must report employee counts, wages, and contributions monthly. This administrative data provides a near-census of formal employment and serves as a benchmark for survey estimates, analogous (in a limited way) to the US QCEW.

### 3.4 Limitations and Gaps

Despite its relative strengths, the Colombian system has significant blind spots:

**Informality remains enormous:** At roughly 47% of employment nationally (and exceeding 60% in some cities and rural areas), nearly half of all work is captured only through household surveys, not administrative data. The informal worker who sells fruit from a cart in Cartagena appears in the GEIH if she happens to be in the sample and answers the interviewer's questions. She appears nowhere else.

**Rural and conflict-zone gaps:** The GEIH's monthly coverage concentrates on the 13 largest metro areas. Rural areas and smaller cities are covered quarterly. Regions affected by armed conflict, coca cultivation, or illegal mining -- which have distinct labor market dynamics -- are hardest to survey and least represented.

**Venezuelan migrant population:** Colombia hosts approximately 2.9 million Venezuelan migrants and refugees (as of UNHCR estimates in recent years), the largest Venezuelan diaspora population of any country. A large share of this population works informally. DANE has made efforts to incorporate the migrant population into the GEIH (and has conducted specific migration modules), but coverage of irregular migrants who avoid official contact remains incomplete. The Estatuto Temporal de Proteccion para Migrantes Venezolanos (ETPV), which began granting temporary protection permits in 2021, has improved the visibility of some of this population, but many remain outside the system.

**Gig economy and platform work:** The rapid growth of platform-based work (Rappi, iFood, Uber, Didi, InDriver) in Colombian cities has created a category of workers who are difficult to classify. They are technically self-employed or independent contractors, but their work is mediated by a platform. The GEIH captures them in the self-employment category, but without distinguishing platform-mediated gig work from traditional self-employment -- an important distinction for understanding labor market transformation.

**Cross-border informal trade:** The Colombia-Venezuela border, particularly the Cucuta-San Antonio del Tachira corridor, supports a massive flow of informal cross-border commerce. Thousands of people cross daily to buy or sell goods, provide services, or transport merchandise. This economic activity involves real work and real income but falls entirely outside employment statistics on both sides of the border.

**Lag for policy response:** A 30-45 day lag is acceptable for academic analysis but too slow for crisis response. When COVID-19 hit in March 2020, DANE's GEIH fieldwork was disrupted, and the April 2020 unemployment spike was not published until June -- by which time the situation had already evolved significantly.

## 4. Reference: How Employment Is Measured in the United States

The US system is included here not as a model for direct replication (its budget and institutional capacity are orders of magnitude beyond what Venezuela or Colombia could field), but as a benchmark for understanding what comprehensive employment measurement looks like and where even a well-resourced system still has gaps.

### 4.1 The Two Primary Surveys

The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) produces the monthly Employment Situation report using two independent surveys:

**Current Population Survey (CPS) -- Household Survey:**
- Approximately 60,000 households surveyed monthly
- Produces the unemployment rate, labor force participation rate, employment-to-population ratio
- Counts people, not jobs (a person with three jobs is one employed person)
- Captures self-employment, agricultural work, and informal employment
- Response rate approximately 82-83%
- 90% confidence interval on monthly employment change: +/- 600,000
- Provides demographic breakdowns (age, sex, race, education)

**Current Employment Statistics (CES) -- Establishment Survey:**
- Approximately 119,000 businesses and 629,000 worksites surveyed monthly
- Produces the headline nonfarm payrolls number (the "jobs number" that moves markets)
- Based on actual payroll records, not self-reports
- 90% confidence interval on monthly change: +/- 110,000
- Provides industry detail, hours worked, and earnings data
- Misses self-employment, agricultural work, and off-the-books employment
- Subject to the birth-death model adjustment for new/closing businesses

### 4.2 Supplementary Data

- **JOLTS (Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey):** ~21,000 establishments, measures job openings, hires, and separations. Released with a 55-day lag.
- **QCEW (Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages):** Near-universe count of employers from unemployment insurance tax records. Covers ~12.2 million establishments. Serves as the annual benchmark for the CES. Six-month lag.
- **Weekly unemployment insurance claims:** Fastest government labor market indicator, five-day lag. Measures flow into unemployment insurance only.
- **ADP National Employment Report:** Private payroll processor data covering ~26 million workers. Released two days before the BLS report.

### 4.3 The Revision Problem

Even the US system, with its enormous sample sizes and institutional capacity, produces data that is substantially revised:

- Monthly CES revisions average 40,000-50,000 jobs in absolute value
- Annual benchmark revisions (CES vs. QCEW) have reached -902,000 (2009), -501,000 (2019), and a preliminary -818,000 (2024)
- The birth-death model -- which estimates net employment from business creation and destruction -- added approximately 904,000 jobs to 2023 totals, sometimes exceeding the reported monthly job gain

The lesson for developing countries: if the best-resourced statistical system in the world still produces estimates that are off by hundreds of thousands of jobs, the notion that Venezuela's sporadic surveys or even Colombia's GEIH are producing precise labor market counts is unrealistic. The question is not how to achieve precision but how to produce useful signals at all.

### 4.4 US Limitations Relevant to Latin America

Several known limitations of US employment measurement are dramatically amplified in developing countries:

| Limitation (US) | Scale in US | Scale in Venezuela/Colombia |
|---|---|---|
| Informal/underground economy | ~5-10% of GDP | 40-60%+ of employment |
| Gig/non-standard work measurement | Growing concern | Dominant mode for millions |
| Geographic granularity lag | Metro data 7-8 weeks late | Sub-national data quarterly or nonexistent |
| Emigration/immigration effects | Manageable adjustment | ~25% of Venezuela's population emigrated |
| Political pressure on statistics | Institutional buffers strong | Institutional buffers weak or absent (Venezuela) |

## 5. The Massive Gap: What Current Measurement Misses

### 5.1 The Informal Economy

The informal economy in Venezuela and Colombia is not a marginal phenomenon to be estimated with a residual calculation. It is the primary labor market for a plurality or majority of workers.

**What informality looks like in practice:**

- Street vending (buhoneros): Hundreds of thousands of Venezuelans and Colombians sell goods on streets, sidewalks, and from their homes. They have no business registration, no fixed employer, and no recorded transactions. Their work is visible to anyone walking through Caracas, Bogota, or Medellin, but invisible to statistics.

- Domestic work: Millions of women (primarily) work as domestic employees -- cleaning, cooking, childcare -- in private households. In Colombia, DANE estimates approximately 600,000-700,000 domestic workers, but the actual number is likely higher. Most work without formal contracts. In Venezuela, the number is unknown.

- Day labor (jornaleros): Construction, agriculture, and odd jobs organized through informal networks. Workers are hired by the day, paid in cash (often in dollars in Venezuela), and have no employment record.

- Motorcycle taxi and informal transport: In Venezuelan and Colombian cities, thousands of people earn their living as motorcycle taxi operators (mototaxistas). Most are unregistered, uninsured, and unrecorded.

- Cross-border pimpineros and trocheros: In the Cucuta-Tachira border region, thousands of people transport goods (and sometimes people) across the border through informal crossings (trochas), bypassing official checkpoints. They earn money, they provide a service, and they are entirely outside any measurement system.

**Why household surveys undercount informality even when they try to measure it:**

- Workers in illegal or quasi-legal activities (contraband, unlicensed services) may not disclose their work to interviewers
- Intermittent workers may not consider themselves "employed" even if they worked during the reference week
- Multi-activity workers may report only their primary activity, understating total informal engagement
- Survey coverage itself is weaker in areas where informality is highest (urban peripheries, rural areas, conflict zones)

### 5.2 Emigration and the Diaspora Workforce

The Venezuelan displacement crisis is the largest in the history of the Western Hemisphere. The UN Refugee Agency (UNHCR) and the IOM (International Organization for Migration) estimate that over 7.7 million Venezuelans have left the country since 2014, out of a pre-crisis population of approximately 30 million. This means roughly one in four Venezuelans no longer lives in Venezuela.

**What this means for employment measurement:**

**In Venezuela:** The labor force shrank dramatically, but the speed and scale of emigration was not tracked in real time. INE population projections, which form the denominator for employment rates, likely lagged reality significantly. If the statistical agency believes the working-age population is 20 million when it is actually 15 million (because 5 million working-age people emigrated), the resulting employment and unemployment rates are meaningless.

Additionally, emigration was selective. The people most likely to leave were younger, more educated, and more employable -- precisely the demographic that was most productive. ENCOVI data has documented this brain drain: Venezuela lost a disproportionate share of its professionals, technicians, and skilled workers. The labor market that remains is systematically different from the one that existed before the crisis, in ways that static surveys cannot easily capture.

**In receiving countries:** Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, Chile, Brazil, and the Dominican Republic absorbed millions of Venezuelan workers, most of whom initially entered the informal labor market. Colombia's DANE has made the most effort to measure this, conducting specific migration modules within the GEIH and publishing separate analyses of Venezuelan migrant employment. Key findings include:

- Venezuelan migrants in Colombia have significantly higher informality rates than native Colombians
- They earn less on average, even controlling for education
- Many are overqualified for the work they perform (the doctor-driving-Uber phenomenon is widespread)
- Their labor market integration varies dramatically by city and sector

But millions of Venezuelans in Colombia lack legal status or have precarious documentation, making them hard to reach through surveys and invisible to administrative data.

**The remittance dimension:** Venezuelans abroad send remittances home -- estimated at $4-5 billion annually by some calculations, though the figure is highly uncertain because much of it flows through informal channels (Zelle transfers, cryptocurrency, physical cash carried by travelers). These remittances represent labor income earned abroad but consumed domestically. They are a massive component of Venezuelan household income that appears in no employment survey.

### 5.3 Underemployment and Survival Work

Conventional unemployment rates in Venezuela and Colombia are misleading because the bar for "employed" is so low. Working one hour in the reference week for any pay counts as employment. In crisis economies, almost everyone does something to survive -- the issue is not whether they work, but whether their work generates a livable income.

DANE's GEIH includes underemployment questions and publishes both "subjective" underemployment (workers who want to change jobs for specific reasons -- insufficient hours, insufficient income, skills mismatch) and "objective" underemployment (a subset who have actively sought to change). In recent years, subjective underemployment in Colombia has ranged from 25% to over 40% of the employed population, depending on the measure and the period. This tells a very different story than the headline unemployment rate of 10-12%.

For Venezuela, underemployment data is scarce, but the qualitative evidence is overwhelming. ENCOVI findings on income poverty among the employed -- where majorities of workers earn below subsistence levels -- suggest that "underemployment" as traditionally defined dramatically understates the problem.

### 5.4 The Youth and Gender Dimensions

Both Venezuela and Colombia face acute youth employment challenges that are poorly measured:

- Youth unemployment in Colombia (ages 14-28) regularly exceeds 20%, roughly double the overall rate
- Young women face compounding disadvantages: higher unemployment and higher informality
- In Venezuela, many young people simply left the country, making domestic youth employment statistics reflect a selected (and shrunken) population

Female labor force participation is measured by both DANE and (sporadically) INE, but the metrics miss critical realities: women's disproportionate burden of unpaid domestic and care work, the concentration of women in the most precarious informal activities, and the impact of the crisis on women's economic agency in Venezuela specifically.

## 6. Modern Data Sources for Latin American Labor Markets

The gap between what is measured and what could be measured is enormous. Several data sources, most of them digital and many already generating data continuously, could provide labor market signals that complement or substitute for traditional surveys.

### 6.1 Job Posting Platforms

**What exists:**
- **Computrabajo** is the dominant job board in Latin America, with extensive listings for Venezuela and Colombia. It covers formal-sector job postings and provides data on demand by sector, location, skill requirement, and offered salary.
- **LinkedIn** has growing penetration in Colombian professional markets, with more limited coverage in Venezuela.
- **Indeed**, **Glassdoor**, and regional platforms like **elempleo.com** (Colombia), **Bumeran**, and **Konzerta** provide additional postings data.
- **Facebook Groups and Marketplace**: In Venezuela particularly, a significant volume of job postings and labor-seeking occurs through Facebook groups, WhatsApp groups, and informal social media channels rather than formal job boards.

**What they could tell us:**
- Real-time demand signals by sector, geography, and skill level
- Offered wage levels (a proxy for market-clearing wages in the formal sector)
- Skills in demand vs. skills available
- Geographic concentration of demand (which cities are hiring, which aren't)

**Limitations:**
- Massive coverage bias: only formal-sector jobs are posted on platforms. The majority of employment in both countries -- street vending, day labor, domestic work, informal construction -- is arranged through personal networks, not job boards.
- Postings do not equal hires. Multiple postings for one position inflate counts.
- Venezuelan platform usage is lower due to internet access constraints and the dominance of informality.

### 6.2 Mobile Phone Activity as Commuting and Work Proxy

**What exists:**
Mobile phone penetration in Venezuela is approximately 70-75% and in Colombia approximately 75-80%, with smartphone adoption growing rapidly in both countries. Mobile network operators (Movistar/Telefonica, Digitel in Venezuela; Claro, Movistar, Tigo in Colombia) generate anonymized, aggregated location data that reveals movement patterns.

**What it could tell us:**
- Commuting patterns as a proxy for employment levels and work location
- Daily and weekly workplace visit counts for commercial, industrial, and retail zones
- Changes in economic activity patterns that precede or accompany labor market shifts
- Regional variation in economic activity at daily frequency

**Existing precedent:** During COVID-19, Google Mobility Reports provided workplace visit data for both Venezuela and Colombia (among many countries). This data showed, in near real-time, the collapse and recovery of workplace activity -- a labor market signal that arrived weeks before any survey data.

**Limitations:**
- Movement does not equal employment. Informal workers may move to the same locations whether they have work or not.
- Data access requires partnerships with mobile operators, raising privacy and legal concerns.
- Coverage gaps in rural areas and among the poorest populations (who may share phones or use basic devices without location services).

### 6.3 Remittance Flows as Emigration and Employment Proxy

**What exists:**
Remittance data is partially tracked through formal channels by central banks and by remittance operators (Western Union, MoneyGram, Remitly). In addition, a growing share of remittances to Venezuela flows through Zelle (person-to-person dollar transfers via US bank accounts), Binance P2P (cryptocurrency-mediated transfers), and physical cash.

**What it could tell us:**
- The volume and geographic pattern of remittances is a proxy for the size and location of the diaspora workforce
- Changes in remittance flows signal changes in diaspora employment conditions (when Venezuelan workers abroad lose jobs, remittances fall)
- Geographic distribution of remittance receipts within Venezuela reveals where the population is and what regions are most dependent on external income
- Remittance-to-GDP ratios indicate the degree to which the domestic economy depends on external labor income

**Limitations:**
- A large share of remittances is informal and untraceable through official channels
- Cryptocurrency-mediated transfers are growing but difficult to attribute to specific countries or individuals
- Remittances measure income transfer, not employment directly -- a single employed diaspora member may send money to multiple family members

### 6.4 Social Media Job-Seeking Behavior

**What exists:**
In both Venezuela and Colombia, social media platforms are used extensively for economic activity:
- **Facebook Groups**: Thousands of groups dedicated to job postings, service offerings, and buy/sell transactions. In Venezuela, groups like "Empleos en Caracas" or "Trabajo en Valencia" have hundreds of thousands of members.
- **Instagram**: Small businesses and independent workers advertise services (food delivery, beauty services, home repair, tutoring) through Instagram accounts and stories.
- **WhatsApp**: Job referrals and informal labor market matching happens extensively through WhatsApp networks.
- **Twitter/X**: Professional job-seeking and labor market discussion, particularly among educated professionals.

**What it could tell us:**
- Volume of job-seeking behavior as a real-time labor demand/supply indicator
- Types of work being sought and offered (revealing labor market composition)
- Informal economy activity levels (service advertising, small business announcements)
- Sentiment about employment conditions

**Limitations:**
- Unstructured data requiring NLP and classification
- Heavy selection bias (younger, more connected populations overrepresented)
- Distinguishing genuine job-seeking from noise is difficult
- Privacy and ethical concerns with scraping personal social media activity

### 6.5 Informal Commerce Platforms and Digital Payments

**What exists:**
- **MercadoLibre**: The dominant e-commerce platform in Latin America, active in both Venezuela and Colombia. Transaction volumes provide economic activity signals.
- **Pago Movil**: Venezuela's interbank mobile payment system, widely used for even small transactions. Transaction volumes and patterns could serve as real-time economic activity proxies.
- **Nequi and Daviplata**: Colombia's leading mobile wallet platforms (owned by Bancolombia and Banco Davivienda respectively), with tens of millions of users. Transaction data reveals consumer spending patterns and P2P economic activity.
- **Binance P2P**: Widely used in Venezuela for dollar-bolivar conversion and as a de facto payment system. Trading volumes correlate with economic activity.
- **Zelle**: Used extensively by Venezuelans for dollar-denominated transactions, both domestically and for remittances.

**What it could tell us:**
- Transaction volumes as a real-time proxy for economic activity and, indirectly, employment
- Geographic patterns of economic activity at granular levels
- Informal economy monetization (when street vendors accept Pago Movil, their activity becomes partially visible through payment data)
- Dollar vs. bolivar transaction patterns revealing the structure of the dual economy

**Limitations:**
- Cash transactions remain significant, especially in the poorest segments
- Transaction data measures spending, not employment directly
- Data access requires cooperation from financial institutions and payment platforms
- Privacy regulation (limited but evolving in both countries) creates constraints

### 6.6 Satellite and Earth Observation Data

**What exists:**
Satellite imagery and nighttime light data (from VIIRS, the Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite) have been used in economic research to estimate economic activity in countries with poor statistical systems.

**What it could tell us:**
- Nighttime light intensity correlates with economic activity and has been used as a GDP proxy for countries with unreliable national accounts (including Venezuela)
- Construction activity visible from satellite imagery proxies construction employment
- Agricultural land use changes signal agricultural employment shifts
- Port activity and shipping container counts proxy trade-related employment

**Existing research:** Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil (2012) demonstrated that nighttime lights data can be used to improve GDP estimates for developing countries. Subsequent research has applied similar methods to Venezuela specifically, using light data to estimate the economic contraction when official GDP figures were unavailable.

**Limitations:**
- Spatial resolution limits the granularity of the signal
- The relationship between light and economic activity is not linear and varies by sector
- Employment cannot be directly inferred from aggregate activity proxies
- Cloud cover and atmospheric conditions introduce noise

## 7. The econOS Opportunity: Labor Market Transparency Where Work Is Invisible

### 7.1 The Problem Restated

In the United States, the employment data problem is one of lag and revision -- the data eventually arrives and is eventually corrected. Payroll processors, job boards, and administrative records provide a rich substrate that could accelerate the process.

In Venezuela, the problem is existential. There is no reliable employment data at all. The official statistical agency does not consistently produce it. The largest independent survey (ENCOVI) is annual and sample-limited. The majority of work happens in an informal economy that no survey or administrative system reaches. A quarter of the population emigrated, invalidating the population base for rate calculations. The currency collapse made nominal wage data meaningless for extended periods. And the people who most need labor market information -- workers deciding whether to stay or migrate, employers trying to hire, migrants trying to find work in receiving countries -- have essentially nothing.

In Colombia, the problem is less severe but still substantial. DANE produces real data, but it arrives with a 30-45 day lag, covers the formal economy much better than the informal one, and struggles to account for the massive Venezuelan migrant workforce operating largely off the books.

### 7.2 What econOS Could Provide

**A composite labor market activity index** that fuses multiple data streams:
- Mobile payment transaction volumes (Pago Movil, Nequi, Daviplata) as real-time economic activity proxy
- Job posting volumes and composition from Computrabajo, elempleo.com, Facebook Groups
- Remittance inflows as diaspora employment proxy
- Mobile phone commuting patterns as workplace activity proxy
- Social media job-seeking intensity as labor market tightness signal
- Nighttime lights and satellite data as economic activity baseline

No single source would be sufficient. The value is in fusion -- combining partial, biased signals from multiple sources into a composite that is more informative than any individual input.

**Real-time informality estimation:**
The gap between total economic activity (proxied by mobile payments, movement data, and satellite observations) and formal employment (visible in PILA in Colombia, or in whatever formal payroll data exists) is itself an estimate of informal economic activity. If mobile payment volumes in a city are growing but formal employment is flat, the growth is likely in informal work. This is crude but directionally useful -- and it would update daily, not annually.

**Emigration and diaspora tracking:**
Combining remittance flow data with social media migration signals, border crossing estimates, and receiving-country registration data could produce a more timely estimate of the Venezuelan diaspora workforce than the periodic UNHCR/IOM coordination platform (R4V) snapshots.

**Skills mismatch visibility:**
Comparing job posting skill requirements with the educational and professional profiles visible on LinkedIn, university enrollment data, and emigration patterns of professionals could reveal where the labor market has structural mismatches -- too many lawyers and not enough electricians, or vice versa.

**Geographic granularity:**
Traditional surveys in Venezuela and Colombia provide national and major-city estimates. Mobile data and payment platforms could provide district-level or even neighborhood-level economic activity signals. For a worker in Maracaibo deciding whether to go to Bogota or Medellin, city-level labor market signals would be transformative.

### 7.3 The Validation Challenge

In the US, alternative data sources can be validated against the BLS benchmark -- imperfect, but a known quantity. In Venezuela, there is no reliable benchmark. How do you validate a real-time employment proxy when the official statistics themselves are unreliable?

Possible approaches:
- **Cross-validation between independent data streams**: If mobile payment volumes, job postings, and commuting patterns all move in the same direction, confidence increases even without an official benchmark.
- **Calibration against ENCOVI**: The annual ENCOVI survey, despite its limitations, provides a periodic anchor point. econOS real-time estimates could be calibrated to ENCOVI levels when available.
- **Calibration against DANE in Colombia**: Colombia's GEIH provides a much stronger benchmark. Models developed and validated in Colombia could be adapted (with caution) for Venezuela.
- **Event validation**: Known economic events (factory closures, emigration waves, policy changes) should produce detectable signals in the data. If a major employer closes and the composite index does not register it, something is wrong.
- **Progressive benchmarking**: As econOS data accumulates, internal consistency checks become possible. Seasonal patterns should recur. Shocks should be visible. Geographic patterns should be spatially coherent.

### 7.4 Why This Matters

The practical consequence of having no labor market data is not an abstract statistical problem. It means:

- A Venezuelan considering whether to undertake a dangerous migration to Colombia, Peru, or Chile cannot assess whether jobs are available at the destination. Migration decisions that affect entire families are made on rumors, WhatsApp messages from friends, and hope.

- A Colombian policy maker designing integration programs for Venezuelan migrants cannot estimate how many are working, in what sectors, at what wage levels, or in which cities. Policy is designed blind.

- An employer in Bogota or Medellin who would hire qualified Venezuelan professionals has no systematic way to find them. Skills matching happens through personal networks, not information systems.

- International organizations allocating humanitarian resources cannot measure whether economic conditions in Venezuela are improving or deteriorating in real time. They rely on annual surveys and projection models.

- Investors assessing Venezuela's potential recovery have no real-time labor market signals. Capital allocation decisions that could accelerate recovery are delayed by information scarcity.

econOS does not solve all of these problems. But it addresses the root cause: the absence of a data layer that makes labor market activity visible. In countries where most work is invisible to statistics, making even a fraction of it visible would be transformative.

## Summary: The Information Void

The contrast between employment measurement in the US and in Venezuela is not a matter of degree -- it is a difference in kind. The US has a system that produces imperfect data with annoying lags. Venezuela has no functioning system at all. Colombia sits somewhere in between: a competent statistical agency doing good work with traditional methods, but facing an informal economy and a migration shock that those methods cannot adequately capture.

The current measurement architecture in all three countries shares a common limitation: it was designed for economies where the majority of work is formal, registered, and visible to administrative or survey systems. In Venezuela and Colombia, this assumption is false. The majority of work is informal, unregistered, and invisible. The measurement system measures the exception, not the rule.

Modern data sources -- mobile payments, job platforms, remittance flows, mobile phone location data, social media activity, satellite imagery -- do not suffer from this limitation. They capture economic activity regardless of whether it is formal or informal, registered or unregistered. They update continuously, not monthly or annually. And they are available now, in countries where traditional statistical infrastructure has failed.

The opportunity for econOS is not to build a better version of the BLS for Latin America. It is to build something fundamentally different: a data infrastructure that makes visible the labor market activity that traditional statistics cannot see. In economies where the majority of work is invisible, this is not an incremental improvement. It is the difference between having information and not having it.
